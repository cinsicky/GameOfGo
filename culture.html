Origin

A 19x19 Go board model from a Sui dynast(581-618 CE)tomb.

Go is an abstract strategy board game for two players, which the aim is to surround more territory than the opponent. The game was invented in China more than 2,500 years ago and believed be the oldest board game continuously played to the present day. Go was originally played on a 17×17 lines grid, but a 19×19 grid became standard by the time of the Tang Dynasty (618–907).

The earliest written reference to the game is generally recognized as the historical annal Zuo Zhuan(c. 4th century BC). It is also mentioned in Book XVII of the Analects of Confucius and in two books written by Mencius (c. 3rd century BC). In the all of those works, the game be referred as yì (弈). Today, in China, it is known as weiqi(simplified Chinese: 围棋; pinyin: About this sound wéiqí), literally "encirclement board game".

Development

Woman Playing Go (Tang Dynasty c. 744), discovered at the Astana Graves

The game reached Japan in the 7th century CE — where it be called go or igo — the game became popular at the Japanese imperial court in the 8th century, and among the public by the 13th century. The modern version of the game as we know it today be formalized in Japan in the 15th century. In 1603, Tokugawa Ieyasu re-established Japan's unified national government.

Go was introduced to Korea sometime between the 5th and 7th centuries CE, and was popular among the higher classes. In Korea, the game be called baduk, and variant of the game called Sunjang baduk be developed by 16th century. Sunjang baduk became the main variant played in Korea until the end of the 19th century, when the current version was reintroduced from Japan.

Despite its widespread popularity in East Asia, Go has been slow to spread to the rest of the world. Go start to become popular in the West until the end of the 19th century, when German scientist Oskar Korschelt wrote a treatise on the ancient Han Chinese game. As of December 2015, the International Go Federation has 75 member countries, with 67 member countries outside East Asia.
